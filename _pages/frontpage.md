## Overview

The **International Training Course on the Atmospheric Boundary Layer and Energy Balance over Glacier and Ocean Surfaces** will take place from **19–23 October 2026** at the **Institute of Oceanography of the Universidade Federal do Rio Grande (IO-FURG), Rio Grande, Brazil**.

The course is organized within the framework of the project **"Extreme Events and Climate Change: Impacts on the Cryosphere from the Tropical to the Polar Regions (TROPIPOLAR-CRYOEXTREMES)"**, a collaborative initiative involving the **[Institute of Oceanography of the Universidade Federal do Rio Grande (IO-FURG), Brazil](https://io.furg.br/)**, the **[Laboratory of Ocean and Atmosphere Studies of the National Institute for Space Research (LOA-INPE), Brazil](https://www.loa-inpe.com.br)**, the **[National Institute of Science and Technology of the Cryosphere (INCT of Cryosphere), Brazil](https://loa-inpe.github.io/inct.html)**, and the **[the Institut des Géosciences de l’Environnement of the Université Grenoble Alpes (IGE, UGA, IRD), France](https://www.ige-grenoble.fr/)**. This activity is supported by the **CAPES–COFECUB Program** (Brazil–France Cooperation Program).

The project seeks to improve our understanding of how climate variability and extreme events, including atmospheric rivers, influence cryospheric systems across a broad range of environments, from the Tropical Andes to the Antarctic Peninsula.

The course is designed for **Master's and PhD students and early-career researchers in Earth Sciences, Atmospheric Sciences, Oceanography, Glaciology, and related disciplines** interested in atmospheric boundary layer processes, surface energy balance, meteorology, and cryosphere–atmosphere interactions.

The training will be conducted in **English, Spanish, and Portuguese**. It will be offered **in person only**, with approximately **10–15 participants**.

The course combines theoretical lectures with practical exercises using real atmospheric and micrometeorological datasets collected over the Southern Ocean and the South Shetland Islands, located in the northern Antarctic Peninsula. To broaden the range of environmental conditions explored, the course also incorporates datasets collected on mid-latitude and tropical mountain glaciers, allowing participants to compare glacier–atmosphere interactions across polar, temperate, and tropical environments.

# Learning Outcomes

By the end of the course, participants will be able to:

* Understand the physical processes governing the atmospheric boundary layer.
* Analyze and interpret surface energy balance components.
* Process and evaluate eddy covariance data.
* Apply Python-based workflows to real micrometeorological datasets.
* Compare boundary layer dynamics across oceanic and cryospheric environments.
* Analyze real-world observations from oceanic and polar field campaigns.

# Real Data: Preprocessing and Analysis

During the training course, participants will work with real micrometeorological datasets collected over the Southern Ocean, the South Shetland Islands in the northern Antarctic Peninsula, and mid-latitude and tropical mountain glaciers. The Antarctic datasets were collected as part of the **Antarctic Modeling Observation System (ATMOS2) Project**, while the mountain glacier datasets were collected by the Institut des Géosciences de l'Environnement (IGE) and the Institut de Recherche pour le Développement (IRD) from long-term observation sites on the Zongo Glacier (Bolivia) and glaciers in the French Alps. Together, these datasets enable participants to compare surface–atmosphere interactions across polar, temperate, and tropical glacier environments.

The data were obtained using eddy covariance systems installed on:

* A meteo-oceanographic buoy deployed in the Bransfield Strait
* A micrometeorological station at Penguin Island
* Meteorological stations installed on Zongo Glacier (Bolivia) and selected glaciers in the French Alps

These datasets include high- and low-frequency measurements of atmospheric variables and turbulent fluxes, allowing detailed analysis of surface–atmosphere interactions in contrasting environments.

The practical component of the course will cover:

* Data preprocessing using [EddyPro®](https://www.licor.com/env/support/EddyPro/topics/eddypro.html), a software package developed by LI-COR Environmental. This processing includes data quality control, coordinate rotation, frequency-response corrections, and turbulent flux computation.
* Post-processing, visualization, and analysis using Python, focusing on:

  * Time series analysis
  * Energy balance closure
  * Flux partitioning
  * Comparison of turbulent fluxes across oceanic, polar glacier, mid-latitude glacier, and tropical glacier environments

This hands-on experience will provide participants with the skills needed to handle, process, and interpret real-world eddy covariance data collected over the ocean and glacier surfaces spanning polar, temperate, and tropical climates.

# Course Schedule

The training course will include:

* **Theoretical Lectures**: Participants will receive a comprehensive introduction to atmospheric boundary layer processes, surface energy balance, turbulence, and atmosphere–surface interactions over oceanic and cryospheric environments. Special attention will be given to stable boundary layers over glaciers and snow-covered surfaces, as well as oceanic boundary layer dynamics.

* **Hands-on Practical Sessions**: Participants will work with real micrometeorological datasets collected during the ATMOS projects in the Bransfield Strait and Penguin Island, as well as datasets from eddy covariance stations on Zongo Glacier (Bolivia) and glaciers in the French Alps collected by the Institut des Géosciences de l'Environnement (IGE) and the Institut de Recherche pour le Développement (IRD). Practical activities will include data preprocessing using [EddyPro®](https://www.licor.com/env/support/EddyPro/topics/eddypro.html), quality control procedures, turbulent flux calculations, and data analysis using Python, enabling comparisons of surface–atmosphere interactions across oceanic, polar, temperate, and tropical glacier environments.

* **Case Studies and Group Discussions**: Participants will analyze energy flux partitioning and atmospheric boundary layer characteristics in contrasting environments, including oceanic and glacier-influenced regions. Results will be discussed collectively to strengthen understanding of the underlying physical processes.

* **Real Data Analysis Project**: Working in small groups, participants will develop and present a short analysis based on real eddy covariance observations, applying the concepts and tools learned throughout the course.
